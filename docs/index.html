Research statement

Financial models depend on transformations applied before estimation. This project studies one such transformation: the Fixed-width Fractional Differencing operator used to construct stationary features from persistent financial time series.

The question is operational. A preprocessing rule should remove non-stationarity without erasing the dependence structure that may carry information. When the volatility environment changes, a single operator calibrated on a long sample may fail on both margins: under-transforming one segment and over-transforming another. The work reframes this trade-off as an operator-selection problem and asks whether the selected order remains stable across volatility regimes defined from VIX.

The contribution is methodological. The object of inference is the stability of the preprocessing operator itself, not the estimation of a latent memory parameter.

d-Invariance of the Fixed-width Fractional Differencing Operator Across Volatility Regimes

Method: nonparametric segmentation, fractional differencing

The fractional differencing operator \((1-L)^d\) generalises integer differencing to non-integer orders (Granger and Joyeux, 1980; Hosking, 1981). Its weights decay rather than terminate, so the operator can reduce non-stationarity while retaining more autocorrelation structure than first differencing. In finite samples, the infinite sequence of weights requires truncation. López de Prado (2018, Chapter 5) gives this implementation through the Fixed-width Fractional Differencing method. The method retains weights up to the largest lag \(L^*\) satisfying \(|\omega_{L^*}(d)| \geq \tau\):

\[ \tilde{X}_t = \sum_{k=0}^{L^*} \omega_k(d) X_{t-k}, \qquad L^* = \max\{k \in \mathbb{N}: |\omega_k(d)| \geq \tau\}. \]

In this design, \(d\) is not estimated inside a predictive model. A deterministic stationarity rule selects \(d\) before model estimation. The selected order therefore depends on the observed series and on the fixed-width specification, including the truncation threshold and the stationarity criterion.

\[ H_0: d_1^* = d_2^* = \cdots = d_m^*, \qquad H_1: \exists\, i,j \text{ such that } d_i^* \neq d_j^*. \]

The hypothesis asks whether the selected order remains invariant across volatility-conditioned training segments when the operator specification is held fixed. The empirical design keeps segmentation and operator selection separate. An external volatility proxy defines training segments. A nonparametric multivariate segmentation procedure supplies internal boundaries (McGonigle and Cho, 2025). A stationary bootstrap evaluates the stability of the selected order across those training segments (Politis and Romano, 1994). Validation and test observations never enter operator selection.

The test is conditional on a supplied volatility-state partition. Regime and segment boundaries define the admissible estimation windows. The selected order is then compared across those windows. Joint inference over boundary estimation and operator selection is outside the public repository scope and is reserved for the publication now in progress.

Missing observations are not forward-filled by default. They are excluded inside each admissible training segment before order selection. This policy avoids introducing artificial persistence through carry-forward imputation.

A public implementation accompanies the project. The repository linked below contains a public-safe implementation of the Fixed-width Fractional Differencing procedure, boundary-consuming segmentation interfaces, tests, and synthetic examples. Results and empirical findings are reserved for the publication now in progress.

Selected references

The references below frame the methodological context of the current project. Each entry corresponds to a primary source consulted directly.

  • Hosking, J. R. M. (1981). Fractional differencing. Biometrika, 68(1), 165–176. Establishes the fractional differencing operator and its autocorrelation properties.
  • Granger, C. W. J., & Joyeux, R. (1980). An introduction to long-memory time series models and fractional differencing. Journal of Time Series Analysis, 1(1), 15–29. Introduces fractional differencing within autoregressive fractionally integrated models.
  • Diebold, F. X., & Inoue, A. (2001). Long memory and regime switching. Journal of Econometrics, 105(1), 131–159. Shows why long-memory behaviour and regime switching can be observationally close.
  • Beran, J., & Terrin, N. (1996). Testing for a change of the long-memory parameter. Biometrika, 83(3), 627–638. Provides a direct precedent for testing stability in memory-related parameters.
  • McGonigle, E. T., & Cho, H. (2025). Nonparametric data segmentation in multivariate time series via joint characteristic functions. Biometrika, 112(2), asaf024. Provides the segmentation method used as an external boundary-generating component.
  • Politis, D. N., & Romano, J. P. (1994). The stationary bootstrap. Journal of the American Statistical Association, 89(428), 1303–1313. Provides the bootstrap procedure used for weakly dependent time series.
  • Tsay, W.-J., & Härdle, W. K. (2009). A generalized ARFIMA process with Markov-switching fractional differencing parameter. Journal of Statistical Computation and Simulation, 79(5), 731–745. Provides a state-dependent fractional-differencing specification and motivates testing whether the selected order varies across regimes.
  • Lipton, A., & Reghai, A. (2023). SPX, VIX and scale-invariant LSV. SSRN Working Paper No. 4356976. Motivates state-aware financial model design and the use of scale-consistent volatility representations.
  • López de Prado, M. (2018). Advances in Financial Machine Learning. Wiley. Chapter 5 introduces the Fixed-width Fractional Differencing method.
